▲ Phantom power is shared

The split is a passive hardwired Y — no transformers. That means 48 V passes to both consoles when either one supplies it.

We phantom the overhead pair. If you'd rather supply it from your side, say so and we'll switch ours off — but one of us needs to, and only one of us should. If anyone on this stage is running a ribbon mic, tell us before anything gets switched on.

24-channel passive split, send only. One leg is yours — take it and use your own preamps; you'll get a clean mic-level feed with none of our gain, EQ or compression on it. The other feeds our XR18.

No transformers means no ground lifts. If a channel hums, it's a ground loop between our rack and your console. We carry inline isolators — flag it and we'll drop one in.

▲ The wired runs are line level

An XR18 aux output is line level, not headphone level. Robert and Rob each need a belt-pack headphone amp at the far end of the run — a Behringer P1 or equivalent. Without one they get a whisper and assume the mix is broken.

All six buses are spoken for. There is no seventh mix. If a guest sits in and needs a wedge, someone gives up their ears for the song.

▲ Clip — never share with lighting

A dimmer pack on the same leg as the rack produces a buzz you will never EQ out. Not with a ground lift, not with a notch filter. The only fix is a different circuit.

Same goes for beer coolers, ice machines, and HVAC compressors. Anything with a motor or a triac.

Get this backwards once and everyone knows

Power Sequence

Sources come up first and go down last. Amps are the opposite.

Power on Rack → Subwoofer → Tops
Power off Tops → Subwoofer → Rack
Why it matters: a mixer powering up or down puts a DC thump on its outputs. If the speakers are already live, the room hears it — and so does the sub's woofer, at full excursion. Speakers are always the last thing on and the first thing off. No exceptions, not even for a quick reboot.
