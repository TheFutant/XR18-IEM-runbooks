00 · Before anyone plugs in

Setup

01 · One person at a time

Gain

Everyone plays or sings at real performance volume. Not a soft test tap, not a polite check-one-two.

▲ Clip — "yellow, kissing orange" is an analog instruction

On a digital console that's roughly −6 to −3 dBFS — 5 to 7 dB too hot. Digital clipping isn't warmth, it's a crack, and it's unrecoverable.

Target: peaks at −12 to −10 dBFS. On the meter that reads green, occasionally touching yellow. If it's living in the yellow, come down.

METHOD → EQ & FX Runbook §00. Read the four-dB-scales warning before you touch a knob — preamp gain, channel meter, fader and GR meter are all labelled in dB and all measure different things.
02 · Now they mean something

Compressor Thresholds

Ratios, attack and release were set Sunday. Thresholds need real signal, so they waited for you.

Pull the threshold down until the GR meter hits the target on the loud moments. Then have them play or sing quietly — GR should barely move. If it's pinned no matter what they do, you've gone too far. Watch the meter, not the number on the knob.

04 · The main event

Six Ear Mixes

This is the part that takes the time, and the part that decides whether anyone enjoys the gig.

▲ Clip — a DCA cannot change an ear mix

Your aux sends tap at Post EQ — before the fader. A DCA moves faders. Therefore a DCA is physically incapable of touching a monitor mix.

There is no "bring up the band DCA as a base" shortcut. Every ear mix is built from its own send levels, channel by channel.

The real shortcut is copy the bus. Build one properly, then paste it to the other five and personalise from there.

Dan — Bus 1
Wireless · build this one first — it becomes the template.
Everyone gets: kick, snare, bass, both guitars, keys, all four vocals, ambience. Get a balanced, usable full-band mix here — then copy it to the other five and adjust one thing at a time with each player standing next to you.
FX: light plate on the vocals
Jamie — Bus 2 · Guitar
Wireless · no mic
Wants his own guitar and the drums. He's not singing, so he needs rhythm lock more than anything else.
FX: none needed
Pappy — Bus 3 · Gtr + Vox
Wireless
Wants his own guitar present and Kevin's lead vocal for timing cues.
FX: light — he's singing
Kevin — Bus 4 · Lead Vocal
Wireless
Wants his own voice well out front, band underneath but not buried. Start his band level lower than everyone else's — a lead singer needs room around their voice.
FX: he needs it most — plate AND slapback
Rob — Bus 5 · Keys + Vox
Wired run
Wants his own two boards and his own vocal clearly above the band.
FX: light — he's singing
Robert — Bus 6 · Drums
Wired run
Wants his own kick and snare well above everything else, plus bass to lock the pocket. He's the clock — he needs to hear himself before he hears anyone.
FX: NONE. Reverb smears the timing reference he's playing to.
▲ Reverb in the ears — per player, not a blanket rule

Singers need something to sing against. A bone-dry vocal in a sealed IEM is unnatural and brutal — they can't hear themselves in a space, so they push, and they're hoarse by the third set.

Drummers need the opposite. Reverb blurs the transient he's timing to.

Send FxRet 1 (plate) and FxRet 3 (slapback) to the vocalists' buses only, around 6–8 dB below their house level. Robert and Jamie get nothing.

05 · When someone complains

Quick Fixes

"I can't hear myself"
Turn them up in their own bus. Never turn everyone else down — that's how you end up with six mixes that are all too loud and nobody can hear anything.
"It sounds thin / lifeless"
Probably a dry ear mix. Add a little plate. Singers especially.
Crackly or distorted
Gain is too hot. Check that channel's meter — if peaks are above −6, come down. This is the single most common cause.
Muting a channel doesn't change the ears
That's the architecture, not a fault. Pre-fader sends tap before the mute. It's a feature — it means your house moves don't wreck anyone's mix — but it surprises people.
DCA does nothing to the ears
Also expected. Same reason. DCAs only move the house.
Wireless dropout
Travel router fighting for Wi-Fi channel space, pack battery, or line of sight. Check in that order.
"Something's just missing"
Right bus, and not muted. Nine times in ten it's one of those two.
