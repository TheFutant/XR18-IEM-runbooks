00 · Before you leave

Load Out

No firmware step. The EON600 line is discontinued and you're not using the app anyway.

01 · Rear panel only

Set Up the 612s

Four things on the back of each box. Identical in both modes.

▲ Clip — the SUB preset is never used

SUB enables a fixed 100 Hz high-pass filter. With no subwoofer, that throws away everything below 100 Hz with nothing to catch it — your bottom end vanishes and you'll hunt the mixer for a fault that isn't there.

And it's still wrong with the sub connected. Your sub crosses at 90 or 120 Hz. There is no 100. The two filters would never meet.

MAIN. Both boxes. Permanently.

▲ Clip — EQ+ hides a curve you can't see

The EQ+ button toggles a saved EQ curve on and off. That curve is only visible in the EON600 app — which you're not running.

If these boxes came from anywhere but a sealed carton, someone may have stored a curve in there. You can't inspect it, but you can switch it off. Confirm the green EQ+ LED is dark on both.

01b · With sub only

Set Up the Sub

The sub owns the crossover. Four cables and three switches.

Main L → IN 1 · Main R → IN 2 · OUT 1 → 612 L · OUT 2 → 612 R
▲ Clip — LINK passes full range

OUTPUTS MODE has two positions and only one of them is a crossover.

LINK = outputs pass full range. Daisy-chain mode. Leave it here and you have a subwoofer, plus two 612s still burning excursion on 45 Hz — you've bought nothing.

XOVER = outputs are high-passed. Check it every load-in. It's a switch, and switches move in vans.

▲ Clip — take 90, not 120

A 12" top carries down to 90 Hz without complaint. At 120 Hz you push enough midbass into the sub that the ear starts to localize it — the low end audibly comes from a box on the floor instead of from the band.

With one centre sub, staying non-directional is the entire point.

Why phase matters: the sub is on the floor, the tops are four feet above it on poles. Their acoustic centres don't line up, so around the crossover they partially cancel. The flip takes thirty seconds and is worth several dB. Most bands never do it.

What this sub doesn't have: delay. Phase 0/180 is the crude alignment tool, not the fine one. Livable — polarity plus placement gets you most of the way — but it's a real gap.
02 · The big win

High-Pass Everything

The 612 is 10 dB down at 48 Hz. Below that it radiates nothing and the cone unloads.

The sub owns everything under 90 Hz. Your job is to make sure only kick and bass get down there.

▲ Technique — stack to 24 dB/oct

The XR18's dedicated channel low-cut is only 12 dB/oct. Set at 45 Hz, it barely touches 41 Hz.

The fix: engage the low-cut at your target, then also set EQ band 1 to LCut type at the same frequency, Q 0.7. They stack to 24 dB/oct.

Only bass and kick. Stack anywhere else and you've burned an EQ band to solve a problem you don't have.

▲ Clip — the overheads are your toms

There are no tom mics on this kit. Kick, snare, and a summed overhead pair — that's the whole drum rig.

So the overhead channel is carrying every tom fill you play. A rack tom's fundamental lives around 100–200 Hz; a floor tom around 60–90. High-pass that channel at 350 and the toms disappear — every fill turns to cardboard.

120 Hz still pulls the kick's fundamental out of the overheads, which is what you actually want: two sources of kick low end arriving microseconds apart is where comb filtering comes from. Toms stay. Kick doesn't double.

Tune by ear: toms thin → come down toward 90–100. Kick flabby and doubled → go up toward 150.

▲ Clip — Rob's keys

Cars is a synth-bass song. You Might Think and In Between Days also lean on synth low end.

If Rob is carrying the bassline on a patch and you blanket-HPF his channels at 80 Hz, you gut the hook. Default piano / organ / pad channels to 60–80 Hz — but if one board is doing bass duty on certain tunes, leave it low and settle with Rob who owns the bottom on which songs.

The 612 is usable to 57 Hz (−3 dB) and reaches 48 Hz (−10) — so your low A at 55 Hz genuinely exists in the room. Your low E at 41.2 Hz does not, and won't without a sub. Filter it out; you're removing something the audience was never going to hear.
The guitar, vocal, overhead and snare filters do not change. They were never a workaround for the missing sub — guitars have nothing useful below 90 Hz and vocals nothing below 100, whatever you play them through. Keeping them filtered is exactly what keeps the sub reproducing kick and bass instead of mud from eleven other channels.

Why it works: the ear reconstructs a missing fundamental from its harmonic series. Play a 41 Hz low E, let the listener hear only 82 / 123 / 164 Hz, and their brain supplies the 41. It's why a phone speaker can carry a bassline.
This section inverts, and that's the whole reason it exists. Everything in subless mode is a coping strategy for a fundamental you cannot produce. Carry those settings into a rig that can produce it and you get a double dose — harmonics and fundamentals, drive and weight. It reads as woolly and undefined, and you'll blame the sub.

Still worth doing, but you can be gentler — the tops aren't burning excursion on 50 Hz any more, so they have real headroom. Start at the lighter end of each range. Cut, don't boost.

▲ Clip — watch the LIMIT LEDs

The 612s each have one. Flickering on peaks is fine. Lit steadily means you have already lost. The fix is upstream — filters, EQ, compression — not more gain.

The 612s and the sub each have one. Flickering on peaks is fine. Lit steadily means you have already lost. The fix is upstream — filters, EQ, compression — not more gain.

If it's the sub's limiter that's pinned, don't reach for the sub's level knob. Compress the bass harder and check that nothing below 90 Hz is arriving from channels that shouldn't have any.

06 · Soundcheck

Tune the Room

100-band RTA on every channel and bus EQ, plus a 31-band GEQ on the Main LR. The only thing you dial on site.

07 · Don't do this twice

Save It

Scope your scenes or they'll undo your afternoon.

Scenes recall preamp gain by default. Recall a quiet scene after you've gained up for a loud room and you'll undo everything. Room tuning must survive every scene change — that's the whole point.

Subless is not a temporary state. Wedding cocktail hours, dinner sets, and ceremony coverage all run tops-only. Both scenes stay in the desk permanently.
