01 · Verified — nothing to do

Send Taps ✓

This one decision governs how the entire rig behaves. It's already correct.

Bus 1–6Post EQ — a pre-fader tap. The six ear mixes ignore the house faders entirely.
FxSnd 1–4Post Fader — reverb follows the fader, so the wet/dry ratio holds when you push a vocal up.
▲ Everything downstream follows from this

The house fader and the ear mixes are completely independent. Push a vocal up for the room and nobody's in-ears change. That's the whole reason to run pre-fader, and it's what makes mixing from stage survivable.

But it cuts the other way: a DCA moves faders, so a DCA cannot touch an ear mix. And the channel mute sits at the fader too — so muting a channel may not remove it from anyone's ears.

Both of those surprise people. Neither is a fault.

02 · Four available

DCA Groups

A shared fader for a group of channels. No audio routes through it.

▲ Clip — don't build a "Band" DCA

The Main LR fader already is one. A DCA containing every channel and the main fader do the same job.

And the XR18 appears to allow only one DCA per channel (mute groups allow several — verify by trying to assign Bass to two). If that holds, a Band DCA containing channels 1–8 makes a Drums DCA impossible — they'd overlap on kick, snare and OH.

You'd have spent your entire DCA budget building a second main fader.

DCA 1Drums — kick, snare, overheads
DCA 2Vox — Kevin, Dan, Rob, Pappy. The one you'll actually grab. Room fills up, four singers vanish under two guitars — one fader, not four.
DCA 3Music — bass, both guitars, both keys
DCA 4Leave empty. Gig with three, then assign it to whatever you keep wishing you had a fader for.
Remember what a DCA can't do: your sends are pre-fader, so DCAs move the house and nothing else. They will never change an ear mix, no matter how far you push them.
03 · Four available, multiple per channel

Mute Groups

An instant kill switch, not a level control.

Mute 1Break — every input. The highest-value one. One tap at the end of a set and every mic on stage goes dead. No open-mic squeal, no stage chatter into the room, no drummer noodling while people order drinks. You'll use it three times a night.
Mute 2FX — all four FX sends. Kill every reverb instantly, for a talk moment or a tiled room that's fighting you.
Mute 3Click — on for the songs that need it, off for the ones that don't.
Mute 4All mics but Kevin — for banter between songs.
▲ Clip — a mute may not reach the ears

The channel mute sits at the fader. Your sends tap before it. So a muted channel may still be present in all six ear mixes.

For Break that's mostly fine — the room goes silent, which is the point. But test it at practice so you learn it there and not mid-set. Mute a guitar and ask Jamie whether anything changed.

05 · Listening tools

Solo, Metering, RTA

The ambience mic is your room RTA. The main-bus analyser shows what the console is sending, not what the room is doing — a bar booming at 60 Hz looks perfectly flat on the main bus. Point the RTA at the ambience channel and you're looking at the actual room. Not calibrated, so absolute numbers mean nothing. Relative is all you need: "there's a hump at 120" is the finding.
06 · The section that saves your afternoon

Scenes, Scope & Backup

Two things in this app are called "Save/Load" and they do completely different jobs.

✗ User Settings → Save/Load

Saves Layers, Layouts, RF Devices, Macros. That's how the app looks — your fader banks and custom pages.

It contains none of your mixer state. No gains, no EQ, no compressors, no routing. Save here believing you've backed up your console and you have backed up nothing.

✓ Mixer view → MS Scenes

The folder icon in the Mixer view. Saves the actual console: gains, filters, EQ, dynamics, routing, FX, DCAs.

This is the one that matters. It also has Scope, Copy, Paste and Share.

▲ Clip — Channel Safes are the wrong tool

Channel Safes protect an entire channel from recall — including its EQ.

Safe your bass and kick to protect their gain, and your GIG / SUB scene can no longer change their EQ or HPF — which is the only thing that scene does. You'd have safed the feature you built.

Use Scope instead. It works per parameter group, not per channel. That's the tool that makes per-song FX recall safe: scope to FX only and a footswitch can flip the slapback without touching a single gain or one dB of room EQ.

▲ The discipline that protects all of it

Load the scene first. Tune second. Never reload.

A full scene recall touches everything, including preamp gain. Bands have wiped an entire setup by loading what they thought was a safe scene. Once you've gain-staged for a room and dialled the GEQ, a recall throws both away.

If you need to change something mid-set, that's a scoped scene — never a full one.

07 · Things that cost real time

Gotchas

LR assignIt's the green LR badge in the channel strip header — same row as +48V, LC, Gate, EQ, COMP. Not in Routing. Routing is physical I/O: which XLR feeds which channel. Un-assign click, talkback and ambience there so they physically cannot reach the room.
EQ freqThe XR18 quantises frequency to a fixed grid. Ask for 50 and it lands on 50.8. That's a quarter of a semitone. There is no setting that changes it, Fine mode doesn't help, and it does not matter. Stop fighting it.
Four dB scalesPreamp gain, channel meter, fader, and GR meter are all labelled in dB, all inches apart, all measuring different things. Gain stage against the channel meter. Nothing else. Full explanation in the EQ & FX Runbook §00.
Fader ≠ meterThe meter is an absolute readout of the signal. The fader is a control that happens to be drawn next to it. A fader at unity says nothing about where the signal is sitting.
FX returnsThey're channels. If FxRet isn't assigned to LR, your reverb goes nowhere and you'll blame the FX engine. 0.0 dB on a return is unity — wide open, not off. Off is −∞.
SlapbackThere's no "slapback" effect. It's a Stereo Delay with Feed L and Feed R at 0%. "Feed" means feedback. Any feedback at all and you've built an echo.
