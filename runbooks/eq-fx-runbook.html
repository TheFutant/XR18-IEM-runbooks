00 · Do this first, and then do part of it again

Gain Staging

Nothing below this line means anything until the gain is right. Compressor thresholds in particular are pure fiction without it.

▲ Clip — there are FOUR dB scales in one channel strip

They sit inches apart, they're all labelled in dB, and they measure completely different things. Read the wrong one and you'll set your gain 7 dB hot without knowing it.

Preamp gain — a slider, −12 to +60. The knob you turn.
Channel meter — counts up toward 0 dBFS. The thing you watch.
Fader — cut/boost, unity at 0. Where you build the mix. Says nothing about signal level.
GR meter — counts down, −1 to −19. The compressor's output, not the channel's.

Gain stage against the channel meter. Nothing else.

Step 1 — Set the gain
Peaks at −12 to −10 dBFS
−12 to −10 dBFS peak not −5 avg lands near −18 to −20
Play it like it's Friday. Everyone sets their gain on a polite check-one-two and then gets buried the moment they actually perform. −5 dBFS peak is not headroom — one harder-than-usual note and you clip the converter, and digital clipping is a crack, not a warmth.
Step 2 — EQ
Everything in §01 below
Step 3 — Set the gain AGAIN
The step everyone skips
EQ boosts add level
A +3 dB boost at 90 Hz makes the channel 3 dB hotter than when you staged it. Your careful −12 dBFS peak is now −9. Go back to the preamp and take it out. This is not optional and it is not obvious — the meter you set is downstream of the EQ you just added.
Step 4 — Compressor threshold
Now it means something
See §02. Watch the GR meter, not the number on the knob.
Step 5 — Fader to unity
Every channel. Then build the mix from there.
If a fader later ends up wildly off — a guitar buried at −20, a vocal shoved to +8 — that's a gain staging error, not a mix decision. Starting everything at unity turns the fader bank into a diagnostic. Starting it anywhere else throws that away.
Housekeeping
Set peak decay to ~2 seconds
Settings → App. A sticky peak-hold marker captures your first bad note and then lies to you for the rest of the session. Auto-decay turns it into a live readout instead of a high-water mark.
Reference — what it actually landed on for the bass: SansAmp on the instrument setting (not line — the house engineer's mic pre expects mic level), preamp around +5, then back down after the 90 Hz lift went in. Compressor threshold ended at −20.5 with +2.0 makeup for ~4 dB of GR.

Those numbers are not a target — they're a sanity check. If your threshold has to go to −40 before the GR meter moves, your gain is too low. If it compresses at −5, it's too hot.
01 · 4 bands per channel

EQ

Q is width. Low Q (0.8–1.2) is broad and musical. High Q (2+) is narrow and surgical. Cut broad, boost narrow — a broad cut sounds like a fix, a broad boost sounds like a mistake.

If a value isn't written here

It's Q 1.0, gain 0, band off. That's the rule. The only exceptions in this document are the vocal Q2.0 notch and the stacked Lo-Cut bands at Q 0.7.

And don't chase exact frequencies. The XR18 quantizes EQ frequency to a fixed grid — ask for 50 and it lands on 50.8. That's a quarter of a semitone. It does not matter, and there is no setting that changes it.

02 · Watch the GR meter, not the dial

Compression

Thresholds depend on your gain staging, so the number on the knob means nothing. Pull the threshold down until the gain reduction meter hits the target on the loud moments. That's the setting.

RMS or Peak — the split runs down the middle of the kit

RMS averages over a short window — it responds to how loud something feels. Use it when the job is smoothing a performance. Peak reacts instantly. Use it when the job is catching a transient before it eats your headroom.

The test: does the source have a violent attack that's much louder than its body? Kick and snare — yes, and that crack is what you're managing. Voice and bass — no. A voice swells, a bass note blooms. Set those to Peak and the compressor lunges at every consonant and pick attack, and the result sounds nervous.

On bass, RMS plus the 25 ms attack is the whole trick: RMS ignores the pluck (too short to move an averaged detector), then clamps the sustain. Punch stays, swell goes. Peak does the exact opposite.

Everything runs Log release and Knee 1. Key On stays off — that's a sidechain and you don't want one.

Bass — the important one
Highest-value compressor in the whole mix
Ratio 4:1 3–6 dB GR Attack 25 ms Release 150 ms RMS
A level bass line can sit higher in the mix without transients slamming the woofer. Medium attack lets the pluck through and catches the sustain. This one earns its keep every single song.
Vocals — all 4
Keeps belted notes from jumping out
Ratio 3:1 4–6 dB GR Attack 15 ms Release 200 ms RMS
Kevin gets more: 4:1, 5–7 dB GR. Verse-to-chorus on a rock lead is 15 dB or more, and nobody's standing at a console riding his fader.
Kick
Evens out hit-to-hit volume
Ratio 4:1 4–6 dB GR Attack 10 ms Release 100 ms Peak
Snare
Ghost notes and hard hits closer together
Ratio 3:1 3–5 dB GR Attack 10 ms Release 150 ms Peak
▲ Clip — don't compress the guitars

They're already compressed — by the amp, by the pedals, by the player's hands. A compressor on a rock guitar channel takes away the dynamic that makes it sound like a rock guitar.

If a guitar is jumping out, that's a fader problem, or a player problem. Not a compressor problem.

03 · Felt, not heard

Reverb

Keep the decay short. A long tail washes out a bar and buries the lyric. Both sends are post-fader.

FX 1 — Vocals
Short room / plate
Decay 1.3 s Pre-delay 20 ms Send −12 dB per vocal
FX 2 — Snare
Shorter, lighter room
Decay 0.9 s Pre-delay 10 ms Send −15 dB
The ears
Put a little verb in the IEM mixes — less than FOH
Singers need something to sing against; a bone-dry ear mix is brutal and makes people push. But a wash in the ears is disorienting and kills timing. Start the FX return around 6–8 dB lower in the aux mixes than in the house. Kevin will want the most.
